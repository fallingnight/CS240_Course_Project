\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}
This project studies influence maximization: choosing a limited seed set whose
diffusion reaches as many network nodes as possible. We connect the application
to monotone submodular maximization, the $(1-1/e)$ greedy guarantee, CELF lazy
evaluation, and a communication-constrained GreeDI-style method. Under
Independent Cascade (IC) simulation, CELF preserves essentially the same spread
as Monte Carlo greedy while reducing average large-preset spread evaluations
from 1026.5 to 266.2 and runtime from 4.67 s to 0.77 s. Distributed candidate
sharing exposes a quality-runtime-communication tradeoff, while weighted degree
is unusually competitive under the weighted-cascade rule.

\section{Background and Problem Formulation}

Influence maximization appears in viral marketing, public-health intervention,
and misinformation analysis. Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos~\cite{kempe2003}
proved NP-hardness under the IC and Linear Threshold models and showed that
expected spread is monotone and submodular. Nemhauser, Wolsey, and
Fisher~\cite{nemhauser1978} therefore imply a $(1-1/e)$ approximation for exact
greedy maximization under a cardinality constraint. CELF~\cite{leskovec2007}
accelerates greedy through lazy marginal-gain evaluation, while
GreeDI~\cite{mirzasoleiman2013} limits global candidate exchange. Later scalable
approaches include TIM/IMM~\cite{tang2014,tang2015}, PaC-IM~\cite{wang2023},
distributed submodular algorithms~\cite{chen2024}, and GreediRIS~\cite{barik2025}.

Let $G=(V,E,p)$ be directed, with activation probability $p_{uv}$ on edge
$(u,v)$. Given budget $k$, the objective is
\[
    \max_{S\subseteq V,\ |S|\leq k} \sigma(S),
\]
where $\sigma(S)$ is expected spread after IC diffusion. Each newly active node
gets one independent attempt to activate every inactive out-neighbor. For
$A\subseteq B$ and $v\notin B$,
\[
    \sigma(A\cup\{v\})-\sigma(A) \geq \sigma(B\cup\{v\})-\sigma(B),
\]
so marginal returns diminish. Exact spread is expensive, and our implementation
estimates it with Monte Carlo simulation.

\section{Representative Methods}

\textbf{Centralized greedy.} Starting from $S_0=\emptyset$, round $t$ selects
\[
    v^\star=\arg\max_{v\in V\setminus S_t}
    \left[\widehat{\sigma}(S_t\cup\{v\})-\widehat{\sigma}(S_t)\right],
    \qquad S_{t+1}=S_t\cup\{v^\star\}.
\]
With $n$ nodes, $m$ edges, and $R$ simulations per estimate, one IC rollout
costs $O(n+m)$ and naive greedy costs approximately $O(knR(n+m))$.

\textbf{CELF lazy greedy.} CELF stores cached marginal gains in a max-priority
queue. Since a stale gain upper-bounds its current value, only the queue leader
is recomputed until the top entry is current for the present seed-set size. It
targets the same greedy sequence with far fewer spread evaluations.

\textbf{GreeDI-style candidate sharing.} Nodes are randomly partitioned among
$m$ workers. Worker $i$ runs local greedy and returns at most $q$ candidates; the
coordinator forms $C=\bigcup_{i=1}^m C_i$ and runs greedy over $C$. We test
$m\in\{2,4,8\}$ and $q\in\{k,2k,5k\}$. Communication is measured by the number
of transmitted candidates.

\section{Implementation and Setup}

The code implements IC simulation; random, weighted-degree, and PageRank
baselines; centralized greedy and CELF; random partitioning and GreeDI-style
merging; graph construction; and CSV/figure generation. The quick preset uses
Erdos--Renyi (ER), Barabasi--Albert (BA), and Watts--Strogatz (WS) graphs with
$n\in\{40,70\}$ plus the 34-node Karate Club graph. The large preset uses the
same synthetic families with $n\in\{50,100,200\}$ plus Karate; $k=10$ for
synthetic graphs and $k=5$ for Karate. Each final seed set is re-evaluated with
a common Monte Carlo seed for the same graph case. Figure~\ref{fig:graph-structures}
shows the quick-preset graph structures.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}

The large preset better reflects the final project state than the quick smoke
test. Table~\ref{tab:overall} summarizes the core methods across ten graph
cases. Ratios are computed relative to centralized greedy on the same graph
after common-seed re-evaluation.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Average core-method performance across all large-preset graph cases.}
\label{tab:overall}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Algorithm & Spread & Ratio & Runtime (s) & Evaluations \\
\midrule
Random & 31.59 & 0.79 & 0.00 & 1.0 \\
Weighted degree & 43.71 & 1.07 & 0.01 & 1.0 \\
PageRank & 35.19 & 0.89 & 0.00 & 1.0 \\
Greedy & 40.43 & 1.00 & 4.67 & 1026.5 \\
CELF & 40.05 & 0.99 & 0.77 & 266.2 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Weighted degree exceeds greedy on average because weighted cascade gives
structurally central nodes high influence potential. This does not contradict
the approximation theorem: the theorem compares exact greedy with the exact
objective, whereas our implementation selects with noisy Monte Carlo estimates
and re-evaluates final seed sets with another common seed. CELF is the clearest
theory-to-practice result: Table~\ref{tab:overall} shows ratio 0.99, 74.1\%
fewer spread evaluations, and an approximately $6.1\times$ runtime reduction
relative to naive greedy.

Communication therefore grows predictably with the budget, whereas solution
quality remains sensitive to estimation noise.

\subsection{Runtime and Evaluation Counts}

Centralized greedy averages 1026.5 spread evaluations on the large preset. CELF
reduces this to 266.2 by reusing marginal-gain upper bounds. Distributed
variants often perform more total evaluations because every worker runs a local
greedy phase and the coordinator runs another phase. These totals are attractive
only when workers run in parallel or when full-graph search is impossible
because of memory, ownership, privacy, or communication constraints. The present
implementation models the distributed structure but does not execute workers in
parallel.

\subsection{Main Findings}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Weighted degree is a strong baseline under the weighted-cascade probability rule.
    \item CELF preserves almost all greedy spread while reducing evaluations by 74.1\% and runtime by about $6.1\times$.
    \item The best average distributed setting, $(2,1)$, matches greedy with a small communication budget.
    \item More transmitted candidates do not guarantee better spread when local and coordinator estimates are noisy.
\end{itemize}

\section{Deviations, Limitations, and Extensions}

The proposal considered synthetic graphs with $n\in\{500,1000,5000\}$ and real
networks such as Wiki-Vote or NetHEPT. The final reproduction stops at 200
synthetic nodes plus Karate so that Monte Carlo greedy runs reliably in the
course environment. This keeps the algorithmic mechanisms transparent but does
not support strong scalability claims. The experiment runner accepts custom
synthetic sizes through \texttt{--sizes}; the submitted CSV files use the
documented quick and large presets.

\textbf{Quality versus structure.} Approximation guarantees alone do not
determine empirical rankings. The graph family and activation-probability rule
can make a simple structural heuristic unusually strong. Greedy remains the
conceptual reference point, but finite-sample spread estimation can alter its
selected sequence.

\textbf{Communication versus estimation.} Increasing $q$ exposes the coordinator
to more candidates and raises selection cost. In principle, the larger union can
improve quality; in practice, it also creates more opportunities for Monte Carlo
estimation error. The non-monotone trend in Table~\ref{tab:distributed} therefore
reflects both communication budget and estimator stability.

\textbf{Reproducibility.} \texttt{simulation.py} implements IC and spread
estimation; \texttt{baselines.py} contains the three baselines;
\texttt{greedy.py} contains greedy and CELF; \texttt{distributed.py} handles
partitions and sharing; \texttt{graphs.py} builds benchmarks; and
\texttt{experiments.py} writes figures and CSV files. All final seed sets for a
graph case are re-evaluated with a common random seed.

\textbf{Extensions.} Useful next steps include deterministic partitioning,
larger real datasets, reverse-reachable-set maximum coverage, additional Monte
Carlo trials, confidence intervals, and true parallel worker execution. These
changes would separate algorithmic work from elapsed-time speedup more clearly.

\section{Conclusion}

Influence maximization provides a compact bridge from modern network
applications to classical algorithm design. IC spread is monotone and
submodular; greedy supplies an approximation guarantee; CELF converts
diminishing returns into a practical acceleration; and GreeDI-style candidate
sharing exposes communication constraints. At course scale, performance depends
not only on the approximation algorithm but also on graph structure,
spread-estimation accuracy, and the candidate-sharing budget.
